Freedom vs Role

A philosophical investigation into moral autonomy, identity performance, and the psychological imprisonment of power in George Orwell’s Shooting an Elephant.

Freedom Expectation Power Home

Freedom as Moral Autonomy

Freedom in Orwell’s essay is not external authority but internal independence. Though armed and symbolically dominant, he lacks the ability to act according to conscience.

“When the white man turns tyrant, it is his own freedom that he destroys.”

Tyranny requires performance. Performance erodes authenticity. Once authenticity collapses, true freedom disappears. Imperialism therefore imprisons both the colonized and the colonizer.

The Coercion of Public Expectation

The elephant is calm. Orwell knows it need not die. Yet the presence of thousands transforms hesitation into obligation. The crowd becomes a silent force of control.

“A sahib has got to act like a sahib.”

Identity dissolves into role. He fears ridicule more than moral failure. Social expectation becomes a subtle but powerful form of coercion.

The Power Paradox

Orwell describes himself as:

“An absurd puppet pushed to and fro by the will of those faces behind.”

The ruler is ruled. Authority becomes dependence. Imperialism demands emotional suppression and constant performance. In sustaining dominance, Orwell sacrifices freedom.

Freedom and the Possibility of Home

Home requires belonging. Belonging requires authenticity. Authenticity requires freedom.

Orwell belongs nowhere — neither to Burma nor fully to the Empire. Because he must perform authority, he cannot exist as his authentic self.

Without freedom, there is no authentic identity. Without identity, there is no belonging. Without belonging, there is no home.

Final Reflection

By shooting the elephant to avoid humiliation, Orwell reveals the deepest irony of power: domination destroys the dominator. Tyranny becomes self-imprisonment.
