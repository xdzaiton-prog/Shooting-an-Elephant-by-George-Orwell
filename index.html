Freedom vs. Role

A Philosophical Reflection on Shooting an Elephant

Introduction: The Paradox of Power

In Shooting an Elephant, George Orwell explores a powerful paradox: Can someone with authority truly be free? Although Orwell holds political power as a British officer in Burma, he reveals that power can destroy freedom rather than protect it. This essay demonstrates that imperial authority creates a psychological prison shaped by expectation, fear, and performance.

What Does Freedom Mean?

“When the white man turns tyrant, it is his own freedom that he destroys.”

In this quotation, freedom does not mean physical strength or legal power. Instead, it means moral autonomy — the ability to act according to one’s conscience. Orwell realizes that by participating in imperialism, he sacrifices his ability to choose freely. He becomes trapped by the expectations of empire.

This reveals that true freedom is internal. Without moral independence, authority becomes meaningless.

Social Expectation as a Prison

Orwell does not want to shoot the elephant once it becomes calm. However, thousands of Burmese spectators are watching him. He feels pressured to perform his role as a decisive imperial officer.

He admits he shoots the elephant simply to avoid looking foolish. This moment proves that public image controls his actions. Although he appears powerful, he is psychologically enslaved.

Authority Without Freedom

Orwell carries a rifle and represents British law. Yet internally, he describes himself as a “hollow, posing dummy.”

This metaphor shows that he is no longer acting as an individual. He is performing a role. The tyrant, therefore, becomes a slave to his own image.

The essay suggests that someone may possess authority while lacking authentic freedom.

Freedom and the Meaning of Home

For a place to feel like home, a person must have autonomy, authenticity, and emotional security. Orwell experiences none of these in Burma. He is hated by the Burmese and conflicted about his role in the empire.

Because he cannot act according to his true values, he feels displaced. The essay ultimately argues that belonging requires freedom. Without moral choice, identity collapses — and home becomes impossible.
