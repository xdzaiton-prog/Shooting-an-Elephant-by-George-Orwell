Freedom vs Role

A philosophical exploration of moral autonomy, identity, and psychological imprisonment in George Orwell’s Shooting an Elephant.

Freedom Expectation Power Paradox Freedom & Home

What Does Freedom Mean?

In Orwell’s essay, freedom is not political dominance but moral autonomy — the ability to act according to conscience rather than performance. Although he holds a rifle and represents imperial authority, he internally lacks choice.

“When the white man turns tyrant, it is his own freedom that he destroys.”

Tyranny demands performance. Performance suppresses authenticity. Once authenticity is suppressed, freedom collapses. Orwell recognizes that imperialism does not only enslave the colonized; it psychologically traps the colonizer in a permanent role.

The Pressure of Public Expectation

The elephant is calm. Orwell knows it does not need to be killed. Yet the presence of two thousand watching faces transforms his situation. The crowd becomes an invisible authority.

“A sahib has got to act like a sahib.”

At this moment, identity collapses into role. He fears humiliation more than moral wrongdoing. Social expectation becomes a form of violence — forcing him to betray his own ethical judgment.

The Power Paradox

Outwardly, Orwell holds authority. Inwardly, he is manipulated. His realization that he is controlled by the crowd reveals the paradox of imperialism: the ruler is ruled.

“I was only an absurd puppet pushed to and fro by the will of those faces behind.”

Authority without freedom is psychological imprisonment. Imperial power requires constant proof of dominance, leaving no room for moral hesitation. The role of Empire becomes a mask that slowly replaces the self.

Why Freedom Is Necessary for Home

Home requires belonging. Belonging requires authenticity. Authenticity requires freedom.

Orwell belongs nowhere. He is hated by the Burmese, yet emotionally detached from the Empire. Because he must constantly perform authority, he cannot exist as his true self.

A place cannot feel like home when identity is reduced to performance. Without freedom, there is no authentic self. Without self, there is no belonging.

Final Reflection

By shooting the elephant simply to avoid humiliation, Orwell demonstrates the tragic irony of power: domination destroys the dominator. Tyranny becomes self-imprisonment, and in losing his freedom, he loses the possibility of home.
