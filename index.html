Freedom vs Role

A philosophical exploration of moral autonomy, identity, and the psychological imprisonment of power in George Orwell’s Shooting an Elephant.

Freedom Expectation Power Home

Freedom as Moral Autonomy

In Orwell’s essay, freedom does not mean control over others. It means control over oneself. Although he carries the rifle and represents the British Empire, he does not possess true choice.

“When the white man turns tyrant, it is his own freedom that he destroys.”

Tyranny forces performance. Performance suppresses authenticity. Once authenticity disappears, moral freedom collapses. Imperialism therefore imprisons both the colonized and the colonizer.

The Violence of Public Expectation

The elephant is calm. Orwell knows it does not need to be killed. Yet two thousand watching faces create psychological pressure. The crowd becomes an invisible authority.

“A sahib has got to act like a sahib.”

Identity collapses into role. He fears humiliation more than moral wrongdoing. Social expectation becomes a form of coercion, forcing him to betray his conscience.

The Power Paradox

Outwardly powerful, inwardly trapped. Orwell describes himself as:

“An absurd puppet pushed to and fro by the will of those faces behind.”

The ruler becomes ruled. The oppressor becomes dependent on the approval of the oppressed. Authority becomes performance. Performance becomes imprisonment.

Imperialism demands constant proof of dominance, leaving no room for moral hesitation. In sustaining power, Orwell sacrifices freedom.

Freedom and the Idea of Home

Home requires belonging. Belonging requires authenticity. Authenticity requires freedom.

Orwell belongs nowhere. He is hated by the Burmese and emotionally detached from the Empire. Because he must constantly perform authority, he cannot exist as his true self.

A place cannot feel like home when identity is reduced to performance. Without freedom, there is no self. Without self, there is no belonging.

Final Reflection

By shooting the elephant simply to avoid humiliation, Orwell demonstrates the tragic irony of power: domination destroys the dominator. Tyranny becomes self-imprisonment, and in losing his freedom, he loses the possibility of home.
